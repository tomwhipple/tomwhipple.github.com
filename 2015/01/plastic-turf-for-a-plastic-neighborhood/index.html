Plastic Turf for a Plastic Neighborhood

Jan 2nd, 2015

The city parks & rec department is proposing to replace the local soccer field with artificial turf.

Once complete, the new field is supposed to be easier to maintain and permit use during and immediately after rain.

However, the plan doesn’t talk about how a huge artificial field will alter the feel of the neighborhood. Today, when it isn’t being used for athletic activites, people are able to walk in & around the field, often with their dogs. In summer evenings, the field cools off quickly after the heat of the day; a refreshing contrast to the surrounding concrete. On winter mornings, a layer of mist can sometimes be seen over the field.

Natural vegitation has a moderating effect on summer temperatures. Artificial surfaces are the opposite. Temperatures on the field during the day could be 10 or more degrees hotter than on a natural field. Obviously this will have an affect on athelete’s health. But the effect on the neighborhood is less obvious. This project will convert a significant part of the neighborhood from natural to an artificial surface. How will this change affect the microclimate of the neighborhood? Will it be even warmer during summer days? Will there be less natural cooling in the evening? I’m glad that I’m already planning to add air conditioning to my house.

When I think about soccer games when I was very young, I think about all the other activity that went on surrounding the games: Sitting on the sideline waiting to play, picking grass & clover. Orange slices at halftime. Soda after the game. Parents & grandparents sitting in lawn chairs watching the game. Families bringing their dogs. Yet all of these things will be prohibited on artificial turf. In summer sun, the turf will be too hot for kids to sit on. It must be protected from food & soda spills. Lawn chairs damage it. In short, the new field will not be capable of supporting the same kind of family atmosphere I grew up with.

And obviously those rules will occasionally be broken. Dogs will be walked on the edges of the field. Kids will spill soda. Geese will fly over. So, special equipment must be purchased to occasionally wash the turf. Yet somehow this is still less maintenance than natural turf.

The research presented by the city doesn’t find much significant evidence for negative helth effects of being in close contact with the rubber & plastic surface as well as it’s chemical byproducts. But there’s not much discussion of runoff of those byproducts.

At the planning meeting, the city distrubuted considerable literature discussing the lack of any evidence for negative helth effects of being in close contact with the rubber & plastic surface as well as it’s chemical byproducts. However, there’s less information about runoff of those byproducts.

The field as it exists today is one of the most beutiful, lushest fields I’ve ever seen.

At the end of the day, there’s a fundamental decision to be made: Do we to be closer to an environment where we’re freely exposed to the benefits and imperfections of natural vegitation? Or do we want to isolate ourselves in a tightly controlled plastic shell?

In my view, we’ve got plenty of isolation & control in our suburbs, shopping centers & offices. Let’s hold onto as much natural vegitation as we can.
